Rate this scan

A functional run from a healthy adult, 3 T GE Discovery MR750, 2D GRE EPI, 3 mm isotropic, 43 slices, 180 volumes, auditory block task. Work through the stages in order — don't skip ahead to the metrics.

1. Look first

On the images alone, how would you rate this run?

No right answer here — that's the point. Most raters land on “poor” or “exclude”: the image is visibly grainy and the brain clearly shifts between volumes. But a visual impression doesn't tell you how much, and how much is what your exclusion criteria are written in terms of. Keep your rating in mind and continue.

The image is grainy and tSNR is only 19. Is low SNR a separate problem from the motion?

Largely a consequence. Motion inflates temporal variance, and tSNR is signal divided by that variance — so a run with 26.7% of volumes exceeding the FD threshold will show depressed tSNR whether or not anything else is wrong. Reporting “low SNR” and “high motion” as two findings double-counts one problem.

  • Not ghosting: GSR is low on both axes. If ghosting were contributing, GSR is the metric that would say so.
  • Not dropout: that would be regional signal loss near air–tissue interfaces. Nothing here localises that way, and dropout isn't a category in MRIQC's BOLD rating widget at all.
  • Not obviously coil failure: coil problems usually produce spatially structured signal loss rather than uniform graininess with high FD.

The general lesson: quality metrics are correlated. To attribute a problem you need the metric that is specific to each candidate — FD for motion, GSR for ghosting — not just a list of numbers that happen to look bad.

3. One more thing

The report also lists these two fields:

spacing_tr (NIfTI header)1.0 s
RepetitionTime (BIDS sidecar)2.0 s

Does this matter?

Yes. The image header and the BIDS sidecar disagree about the repetition time by a factor of two. One of them is wrong, and anything that trusts the header — slice-timing correction, temporal filtering, the frequency axis of any spectral analysis — will be silently wrong along with it.

This isn't an artifact in the image; it's a curation error. It is also invisible unless you read the header, which is why “get to know your data” checks belong before visual inspection rather than after. Fix or verify against the sequence before processing, and check whether it affects the whole dataset or just this subject.

What this case teaches

  • “Looks bad” is a prompt to investigate, not a diagnosis.
  • Metrics are correlated; use the artifact-specific one to attribute cause.
  • The most consequential problem in a dataset may not be visible in the image at all.
