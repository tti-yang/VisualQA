New to this topic?

Start with Zhang et al. (2023) for a map of common EEG analysis methods. Then use Jas et al. (2018) to see a complete, reproducible analysis workflow. The other resources focus on preprocessing choices, statistics, reporting, and inclusive data collection.

2023 Preprocessing pipelines

EEG is better left alone

Delorme, A. Scientific Reports, 13, 2372.

What it explains: A comparison of automated EEG preprocessing choices and optimized pipelines in EEGLAB, FieldTrip, MNE, and Brainstorm. It asks whether more cleaning always produces better ERP data.

Look at: The data-quality metric, the comparison of individual preprocessing steps, and the final software-specific pipelines. Treat the conclusions as evidence for these datasets and metrics, not as one rule for every study.

2025 Preprocessing & decoding

How EEG preprocessing shapes decoding performance

Kessler, R., Enge, A., & Skeide, M. A. Communications Biology, 8, 1039.

What it explains: How filtering, referencing, baseline correction, detrending, and artifact handling change EEG decoding results across 2,592 preprocessing paths.

Look at: Figure 1 for the preprocessing “multiverse,” the results for EEGNet versus time-resolved decoding, and the warning that high decoding accuracy can come from condition-related artifacts rather than neural information.

2023 Recommended overview

The applied principles of EEG analysis methods in neuroscience and clinical neurology

Zhang, H., Zhou, Q. Q., Chen, H., Hu, X. Q., Li, W. G., Bai, Y., Han, J. X., Wang, Y., Liang, Z. H., Chen, D., Cong, F. Y., Yan, J. Q., & Li, X. L. Military Medical Research, 10, 67.

What it explains: A broad introduction to power-spectrum, time–frequency, connectivity, source-localization, and machine-learning approaches, with examples from neuroscience and clinical neurology.

Look at: The overview figures and comparison tables. Use them to connect a research question to a family of methods before studying the mathematical details.

2018 Reproducible workflow

A reproducible MEG/EEG group study with the MNE software

Jas, M., Larson, E., Engemann, D. A., Leppäkangas, J., Taulu, S., Hämäläinen, M., & Gramfort, A. Frontiers in Neuroscience, 12, 530.

What it explains: A complete, reproducible group-level M/EEG analysis using an open dataset and MNE, from preprocessing and quality checks to sensor analysis, source localization, and statistics.

Look at: The quality-assessment examples, the alternative choices discussed at each stage, and the openly available code. This is especially useful for seeing how separate methods fit into one workflow.

2025 Reporting checklist

The GREENBEAN checklist for reporting studies evaluating EEG-based biomarkers

Ewen, J. B., Babiloni, C., Collins, G. S., Ethridge, L. E., Gotman, J., Ikeda, A., Karoly, P. J., Potter, W. Z., Rampp, S., Seeck, M., & Beniczky, S. Clinical Neurophysiology, 176, 2110777.

What it explains: A reporting checklist for studies that evaluate EEG-based diagnostic, prognostic, predictive, or monitoring biomarkers.

Look at: The checklist table and its sections on participant selection, acquisition, preprocessing, model evaluation, and transparent reporting. Use it while planning a study, not only when writing the paper.

2024 Inclusive data collection

The effect of hair type and texture on EEG and ERP data quality

Lees, T., Ram, N., Swingler, M. M., & Gatzke-Kopp, L. M. Psychophysiology, 61(3), e14499.

What it explains: How hair texture and volume relate to EEG/ERP data quality and participation, using measures from acquisition through final ERP variables.

Look at: The signal-quality measures, the gel-volume analyses, and the practical recommendations for collecting usable data without excluding people based on hair type or style.

2025 Practical guide

EEG hair-types infographic

Society for Psychophysiological Research, Committee to Promote Student Interests. Practical inclusivity resource.

What it explains: A short visual guide to making EEG sessions more inclusive for participants with different hair types and styles.

Look at: The practical preparation and communication suggestions. It is a quick companion to the Lees et al. paper above and can be shared with research teams.

2017 ERP statistics

How to get statistically significant effects in any ERP experiment (and why you shouldn’t)

Luck, S. J., & Gaspelin, N. Psychophysiology, 54(1), 146–157.

What it explains: How choosing electrodes or time windows after seeing the data, and running many related statistical tests, can produce convincing but false-positive ERP effects.

Look at: The simulations, the familywise and experimentwise error examples, and the recommendations to define measurements in advance and simplify statistical tests.
