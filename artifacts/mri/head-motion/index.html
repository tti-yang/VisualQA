Head motion

Blurring and concentric ringing on structural scans, most visible at high-contrast boundaries; on functional scans, the brain visibly shifts position between volumes. It is the most consequential artifact in this catalogue, and the one least well handled by looking at pictures — motion small enough to pass visual inspection still biases the numbers you extract afterwards, and it does so in the direction of your hypothesis more often than chance.

The call, in short

  • Ringing confined to skull and scalp, gray/white boundary intact → usable for volumetrics; re-inspect before surface-based analysis.
  • Ringing reaching cortex, or blurred gray/white boundary → run the segmentation and judge the surfaces, not the raw image.
  • Functional: check framewise displacement before eyeballing. Decide against your pre-registered threshold, not against these examples.
  • Set the threshold before you look. Motion is correlated with age, diagnosis, symptom severity, and body size, so a threshold chosen after seeing the data can silently become a threshold on the variable you are studying.
  • Passing inspection is not the same as being unaffected. See "Motion is a confound, not only an artifact" below — this is the section that matters most on this page.
  • Participant still in the scanner? Rescan. Nothing below beats that.

Not to be confused with

  • Gibbs (truncation) ringing — the most common misidentification. Gibbs ringing is regular, evenly spaced, runs parallel to a high-contrast boundary, decays within a few voxels of it, and is present in perfectly still scans; it is a consequence of finite k-space sampling, not of the participant. Motion ringing is irregularly spaced, travels further into the image, and comes with blurring that Gibbs does not produce.
  • Eye spillover and vessel pulsation — streaks that emanate from a specific structure along the phase-encode axis, rather than ringing that radiates from every high-contrast edge. They co-occur with motion often enough that finding one is a reason to look for the other.
  • Aliasing ghost — a discrete displaced copy of the head at a half-FOV offset, not a graded ripple.
  • Susceptibility artifact — stationary signal loss at air–tissue interfaces. It does not vary across a functional run; motion does. If a dark region breathes over time, that is motion, or motion interacting with the field.
  • Bias field / intensity non-uniformity — smooth, low-spatial-frequency shading across the image with no ringing and no edge blurring.

Where to look

On structural scans, inspect the background as well as the brain: motion propagates ripples into the air around the head, where nothing should be. Then inspect the gray/white boundary at high zoom, which is where blurring becomes decision-relevant. Among MRIQC's image quality metrics, the coefficient of joint variation and the entropy focus criterion are the ones that respond most directly to motion-induced blurring and ghosting.2 Crucially, if the scan is destined for morphometry, judge the segmentation output rather than the raw image — a scan can look tolerable and still yield displaced surfaces.

On functional scans, the numbers come first and the pictures second. Framewise displacement and DVARS traces identify which volumes are affected and when; the carpet plot shows whether those events produced brain-wide signal changes, which is what actually contaminates your analysis.2 A run with a handful of isolated spikes is a different problem from one with continuous drift, and the two call for different remedies even at identical mean framewise displacement.

Reading these together is what distinguishes the two panels. FD is computed from the realignment parameters and reports that the head moved; DVARS is computed from the image data and reports that the signal changed. A spike in both is a motion event worth acting on. A spike in FD alone means the movement did not appreciably perturb the signal, and censoring on FD would discard a usable volume; a spike in DVARS alone points somewhere other than motion.

Motion is a confound, not only an artifact

Everything above treats motion as something that makes images look wrong. The larger problem is that it makes results come out wrong, in ways that survive visual QC.

On structural data, motion during acquisition systematically reduces estimated gray matter volume and cortical thickness — that is, it looks like atrophy.3 The effect is present at motion levels too subtle to flag by eye.4 Any study comparing a group that moves more against a group that moves less is therefore at risk of recovering a thickness difference that is entirely artifactual.

On functional data, motion introduces distance-dependent bias into connectivity estimates: correlations between nearby regions are inflated and correlations between distant regions are reduced, producing exactly the pattern many developmental and clinical hypotheses predict.5,6 Because children move more than adults, older adults more than younger, and patients more than controls, motion is confounded with the grouping variable in a large fraction of the literature.7 Denoising strategies differ substantially in how well they suppress this, and none removes it entirely.8

The practical implications for QC are three. Treat motion as a measured variable, not merely an inclusion gate — retain it and report it. Test whether it differs across your groups, and say so if it does. And apply identical criteria to every group: exclusion thresholds applied after inspection can themselves induce the bias they were meant to prevent, because excluding high-motion participants preferentially removes one group.

Calibrating your eye

Visual rating of motion has poor inter-rater reliability, and the usual reason is that raters have never seen the same brain with and without it. The MR-ART dataset addresses this directly: 148 participants scanned in still and deliberately-moving conditions, with expert quality scores and MRIQC metrics included.9 Working through a handful of matched pairs is the fastest way to learn where your own threshold sits, and to discover how much motion passes unnoticed.

Severity ladder

AppearanceCallReasoning
No visible ringing; sharp gray/white boundary; functional traces below threshold Include Still record the motion estimates as covariates. "Passed" is not "unaffected".
Faint ringing over skull and scalp only, boundary intact Include Acceptable for volumetrics; re-inspect after surface reconstruction.
Ringing reaching cortex, or gray/white boundary visibly softened Uncertain Judge the segmentation, not the image. Morphometric estimates from this scan are likely biased downward.
Functional run with isolated high-displacement volumes, otherwise stable Uncertain Potentially recoverable by censoring, depending on how many volumes remain and on your pre-specified strategy.
Continuous drift or repeated large displacements through the run Exclude Censoring cannot rescue a run in which most volumes are affected, and the residual bias is systematic rather than random.
Structure unidentifiable; boundaries lost Exclude No downstream step recovers this. Rescan if the participant is still available.
