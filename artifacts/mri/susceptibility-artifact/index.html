Susceptibility artifact

Where air meets tissue — above the sinuses, beside the ear canals — the local magnetic field is warped. Gradient-echo EPI responds in two ways: it loses signal outright (dropout) and it misplaces the signal that survives along the phase-encode axis (distortion). Orbitofrontal cortex and the anterior and inferior temporal lobes bear the worst of it. Unlike most entries in this catalogue, this artifact is not a failure of the scan; it is a property of the sequence and the skull, and it is present in essentially every EPI dataset. The question is never whether it is there, but whether it lands on the tissue you care about.

The call, in short

  • Presence is not the finding; overlap is. Locate the void relative to your ROI. Dropout over OFC is fatal for a reward or olfaction study and irrelevant for a motor one.
  • Dropout cannot be recovered downstream. No preprocessing step restores signal that was never encoded. Distortion, by contrast, is largely correctable given a fieldmap or opposite phase-encode acquisition — do not treat the two as one problem.
  • Extent consistent with the rest of the sample → document the coverage and move on. This is the expected state of gradient-echo EPI.
  • Markedly larger, asymmetric, or newly present relative to the same participant's other runs → investigate. Head rotation, sinus congestion, and orthodontic hardware all move the boundary.
  • A void with straight edges, or one that does not sit at a known air–tissue interface → this is probably not susceptibility. See below.
  • Judge on the mean EPI, not a single volume, and judge before normalisation — warping to template smears the void's true extent.

Not to be confused with

  • Incomplete slab coverage — also produces missing tissue, but the boundary is a straight plane parallel to the slice stack, typically clipping the vertex or cerebellum. Susceptibility dropout has soft, anatomically shaped edges that fade rather than cut.
  • Metal artifact — dental work, retainers, and implants produce the same physics at far greater magnitude, with gross geometric warping and often a bright rim adjacent to the void. Suspect this when the affected region is unusually large or sits near the mouth rather than above the orbits.
  • Head motion — varies across the run; susceptibility dropout is stationary. If the dark region breathes over time, it is motion, or the interaction between motion and the field — not dropout alone.
  • Coil failure or receive inhomogeneity — signal loss follows the geometry of the coil elements, usually a smooth peripheral falloff, not the skull base.

Dropout and distortion are one cause with two faces

Both arise from the same static field inhomogeneity, but through different routes, and they respond to different remedies.

Dropout is intravoxel dephasing. Within a single voxel straddling a field gradient, spins precess at different rates and cancel by the time the echo is read. It scales with echo time and with voxel volume, which is why shorter TE, thinner slices, and smaller voxels recover signal.3 Slice orientation matters too: tilting the transverse slab towards coronal can substantially restore orbitofrontal signal.4 All of these are acquisition-time decisions. Once the data exist, the signal is gone.

Distortion is mispositioning. Because EPI samples the phase-encode direction with very low bandwidth, a local field offset translates into a large spatial displacement along that one axis — stretching in one place, piling up in another. This is retrospectively correctable, either from an acquired fieldmap or from a pair of EPI images with opposite phase-encode polarity.5 Modern pipelines implement this as a standard step.6

The practical consequence for QC: a run with correctable distortion and a run with severe dropout over your ROI are not equally salvageable, and should not receive the same rating.

Where to look

The clearest demonstration is the one in the figure above — load the mean or a representative BOLD volume over the participant's T1w and toggle the overlay. Tissue that is present in one image and absent in the other is unambiguous, in a way that staring at the EPI alone is not. Hold brightness and contrast fixed while toggling, or the demonstration proves nothing.

Beyond that: the temporal SNR map shows the affected regions as low-value zones extending beyond the visually obvious void, and is the better basis for a quantitative call. In fMRIPrep reports, the BOLD-to-T1w registration panel with anatomical contours overlaid makes the missing territory legible, and the susceptibility distortion correction panel shows the before/after for the distortion component specifically.7 If your ROI is small and sits near a known interface, an explicit coverage check — what fraction of ROI voxels exceed a signal threshold in each participant — is worth more than any visual rating.

A slice mosaic makes the same point at a glance, and has the advantage of showing the full inferior–superior extent rather than one plane at a time. The MRIQC visual report generates exactly this view for every functional run, which is where most people will first encounter the artifact; the protocol of Hagen et al. is the reference for reading those reports systematically.8

Severity ladder

AppearanceCallReasoning
Dropout limited to orbitofrontal cortex and temporal poles; ROI elsewhere Include The expected baseline for gradient-echo EPI. Record the coverage; no action needed.
Visible distortion near sinuses, dropout comparable to the rest of the sample, fieldmap available Include The correctable component is correctable. Verify the SDC panel actually improved alignment.
Void edge grazes the ROI, or ROI coverage is partial Uncertain Quantify coverage rather than eyeballing it, and check whether the affected participants differ systematically by group.
Extent substantially exceeds the sample norm, or is grossly asymmetric Uncertain Points to head position, hardware, or metal rather than ordinary anatomy. Identify the cause before deciding.
ROI sits inside the void Exclude There is no measurement to analyse. Note that this excludes the region, not necessarily the participant — and if it holds across the sample, it is a design problem, not a QC one.
