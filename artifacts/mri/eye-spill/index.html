Eye spillover

Signal from moving and blinking eyes propagates along the phase-encode axis, producing streaks or faint replicas of the orbits that extend across the image and into the background. Whether it matters depends almost entirely on which direction the phase-encode axis points.

The call, in short

  • Check the phase-encode direction first. Axial brain scans are usually phase-encoded left-right precisely so eye artifacts travel across empty space rather than through the brain. If yours is anterior-posterior, the spillover runs into the inferior occipital lobes and is far more damaging.
  • Streaks confined to background, not crossing brain tissue → note it and move on. Structure in the background is not itself the problem.
  • Streaks crossing brain tissue → those voxels carry contaminating signal. Judge against your ROI: spillover through occipital cortex matters enormously for a visual study and less for a frontal one.
  • Present across a whole session or site → this is a prescription issue. Check the phase-encode direction in the protocol before more data is collected.
  • If your data are defaced, you may not be able to assess this at all — see below.

Not to be confused with

  • Vessel pulsation — travels along the same phase-encode axis by the same mechanism, and often appears in the same image. Distinguish by origin: trace the streak back to the orbits or to a vessel. Both are shown in the figure above.
  • Aliasing ghost — a discrete displaced copy of the whole head offset by half the FOV, rather than streaks emanating from a specific structure.
  • Head motion — ringing radiates from all high-contrast boundaries, not from the eyes specifically. The two frequently co-occur, since a participant who moves also blinks.

Where to look

Because no signal originates from the air around the head, the background should be empty; visible structure there means something has spilled into it. Spillover is most clearly seen in MRIQC's background noise panel, and is frequently detectable in the standard deviation map as well.2 Inspecting the background rather than only the brain is the habit that catches this artifact.

The defacing problem

Defacing — zeroing the voxels around the face before data sharing — removes the orbits along with the identifying features. That means the visual evidence for eye spillover is deleted from the image, and quality metrics sensitive to artifacts in that region become uninformative.3 Since most openly shared structural data are defaced, this artifact is often simply unassessable in public datasets, and the affected metrics should be identified and excluded rather than trusted.

Practical consequence: assess eye spillover on your own data before defacing, and record the rating alongside the shared data. Once it is defaced, that judgment can no longer be made or reproduced by anyone downstream.

Severity ladder

AppearanceCallReasoning
No visible streaking from the orbits Include Nothing to act on.
Streaks in background only, phase-encode left-right Include Signal is spilling into empty space, as the protocol intends.
Streaks faintly overlapping brain edge Uncertain Check whether the affected region overlaps your ROI, and inspect segmentation output.
Clear streaking through brain tissue, especially occipital with anterior-posterior phase-encoding Exclude Contaminated voxels in a region likely to matter; degradation is substantially more troublesome on this axis.
